Biography

A scholar of modernism, exile, and the lives women wrote — and did not get to write.

Mary Lynn Broe is the Caroline Werner Gannett Professor of Humanities at the Rochester Institute of Technology, an appointment created to explore the intersections of the humanities with the social sciences, sciences, and technology — both across campus and nationally.

Across three decades she has been one of the defining voices in the recovery and reevaluation of women modernists. Her landmark volumes on Djuna Barnes and Sylvia Plath, and her pathbreaking work on women's writing in exile, reshaped how a generation of readers understood the archive, the family, and the politics of the page.

Before RIT she held the Louise R. Noun Endowed Chair in Women's Studies and English at Grinnell College for sixteen years, where she introduced gender and women's studies into the liberal arts curriculum, and taught earlier at SUNY-Binghamton and the University of Notre Dame. Her scholarship has been carried on fellowships from the Getty, the Ford and Rockefeller Foundations, the NEH, and the ACLS.

She is also a working poet. As she puts it in "Introit": "I write heartbreak torch songs, full of / Unabashed yearning. Also / Celtic rants… / No one hearing my poetry / Would ever mistake / Improvisation / For metric calisthenics."

Books

Selected volumes

Silence and Power: A Reevaluation of Djuna Barnes

London & Carbondale: Southern Illinois University Press, 1991 · Editor

The most important volume to date on Djuna Barnes — more than a collection of criticism, it captures the eclectic character of the woman herself, with personal reminiscences, previously unpublished letters, scattered memoirs, and abundant prints of Barnes's sketches and paintings.

"…the most important volume to date on Djuna Barnes… It includes abundant, well-selected photographs and prints of Barnes's sketches and paintings, which clarify the essays."Bonnie Kime Scott, Signs, Winter 1993
"These essays, lightning bolts and laser beams of critical intelligence, will cauterize and change our perceptions of Barnes and cultural modernism."Catharine Stimpson, from the Afterword

Women's Writing in Exile

Chapel Hill: University of North Carolina Press, 1989 · Co-edited with Angela Ingram

Sixteen essays exploring the varieties of exile women writers in Western culture have experienced over the last hundred years — exile both geographic and imposed, as much metaphor as material circumstance — on Djuna Barnes, H.D., Alice Walker, Isak Dinesen, Jean Rhys, Nadine Gordimer, Doris Lessing, Sylvia Townsend Warner, and Virginia Woolf. The volume includes Broe's pathbreaking essay, "My Art Belongs to Daddy: The Textual Economics of Hayford Hall."

"Rooted in evocations of struggle, this work proposes 'exile' as a political reality and a political metaphor… A striking, salutary collection for feminist scholarship in its historical and cultural reconsideration of modernism."Rachel Blau DuPlessis, Temple University

Protean Poetic: The Poetry of Sylvia Plath

London & Columbia, MO: University of Missouri Press, 1980

An early, career-spanning study that reads Plath beyond the "confessional" label, attending to the work rather than the life and death. Excerpts reprinted in Sylvia Plath: Modern Critical Views, ed. Harold Bloom (Chelsea House, 1989), and in Making Literature Matter, ed. Schilb & Clifford (St. Martin's/Bedford, 2006).

Camminare nel Buio (Black Walking)

Selected Letters of Djuna Barnes to Emily Holmes Coleman, 1934–38 · Archinto, Milan, 2004 · German edition: Im Dunkeln gehn, Klaus Wagenbach, Berlin, 2002 · English edition forthcoming

The selected correspondence between Djuna Barnes and Emily Holmes Coleman across the crucial years 1934–38 — a window onto New York, London, and Paris, and onto the completion of Barnes's most famous work, Nightwood. A record of two brilliant, complicated women who gave each other license to search the depths of the intimate.

Courses

A selection of courses

01

Autobiography: Outlaw Narratives

0504-490-01

We live, said James Merrill, in the age of "me-moir." This course explores the quarrel between truth and fiction across a rich spectrum of life-writings — from formal autobiography to "outlaw" narratives, diaries, ethnographies, and visual self-portraits in art, photography, and film — tracing new modes of constructing identity across race, nation, class, and gender.

02

Maps, Spaces and Places

504-493-01

Space speaks. Beginning with a meditation on the language of maps — antique and digital — the course explores the idea that to tell a useful and truthful picture, an accurate map must tell lies. It treats spatial thinking as critical across the STEM disciplines and culminates in a project reading "RIT or Rochester as Text."

03

The Novel: Banned, Burned & Transgressive

0504-443-01

The origin and rise of "the novel" alongside the censorship history of creative works over four centuries — from Milton's Areopagitica and Galileo's Dialogue through Joyce's Ulysses, Henry Miller, and Rushdie's Satanic Verses — with visual materials by Cadmus, Mapplethorpe, and others.

04

The Art of Poetry

0504-441-01

From formal verse to online, performance, and hip-hop poetry — Hopkins and Hardy to the confessionals, contemporary women poets, and environmental poets. The aim: to read for "the world within the word," to learn interpretive strategies that bring pleasure and social awareness, and to move poetry outside the classroom.

05

New Cartographies: Interdisciplinary Negotiations

COLA 0520 / COS 1055 · Caroline Werner Gannett Project

As digital mapping evolves, mapmaking begins to emphasize features as an artist might paint a picture. Working across environmental and imaging sciences, art, and literature, the course opens an interdisciplinary "contact zone" exploring how maps generate creativity and cultural change.

06

Truth and Consequences: Disciplinary Evidence

COS / COLA · Caroline Werner Gannett Project

A cross-disciplinary look at what evidence is — and how it is collected, sifted, evaluated, and preserved across legal, criminal, historical, scientific, journalistic, medical, geographic, and aesthetic realms. Over a dozen RIT and visiting faculty shape the questions.
